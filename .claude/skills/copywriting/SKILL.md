# Copywriting — Clarity, Precision, Persuasion

Good copy is not flowery. It is not impressive. It is not long. Good copy does one thing: it moves a reader from where they are to where you need them to be, as fast as possible.

## The Hierarchy of Copy Quality

From worst to best:

1. **Verbose and vague** — "We leverage cutting-edge artificial intelligence technologies to deliver unprecedented clinical decision support capabilities."
2. **Clear but flat** — "We use AI to help doctors find medical evidence."
3. **Clear and specific** — "Send any clinical question. Get a cited answer in 10 seconds."
4. **Clear, specific, and unexpected** — "No app. No login. Just WhatsApp — where doctors already live."

Always aim for level 4. Most first drafts are level 1.

---

## Core Rules

### 1. Cut in Half

Every first draft is twice as long as it should be. Cut every sentence by 30%. Then cut again.

```
BEFORE: "In the context of our medical evidence retrieval system, it is important to 
note that citations are verified against their original source material through an 
automated process that ensures accuracy."

AFTER: "Every citation is verified against its source before delivery."
```

### 2. Concrete Over Abstract

Replace abstract nouns with specific things doctors can picture.

```
BEFORE: "Enhanced evidence quality through systematic verification processes"
AFTER: "Every number in every answer traces to a real paper you can click"
```

### 3. Active Over Passive

```
BEFORE: "Guidelines are retrieved from ICMR and verified by the system"
AFTER: "Noocyte retrieves ICMR guidelines and verifies every claim"
```

### 4. You (the reader) Over We (us)

```
BEFORE: "We have built a platform that enables physicians to access..."
AFTER: "You get evidence-based answers, cited, in 10 seconds."
```

### 5. One Idea Per Sentence

If a sentence contains "and" or "but" or "while", split it.

## Audience Adaptation

### Writing for Doctors (Noocyte's primary audience)

Doctors are skeptical, time-poor, and evidence-trained. They can smell bullshit from 10 meters.

**What they hate:**
- Vague claims without evidence ("advanced AI technology")
- Overpromising ("replaces clinical judgment")
- Prescribing language from an AI ("this patient needs X")
- Walls of text

**What they respond to:**
- Specific citations and numbers ("IDSA 2021 recommends fidaxomicin over vancomycin — here's why")
- Peer validation ("used by 1,200 doctors across 8 Indian states")
- Speed ("answer in 10 seconds, not 10 minutes")
- Transparency ("here's the source, judge it yourself")

**Template for doctor-facing copy:**
```
Hook: [Specific problem they face right now]
Evidence: [What the data says — not what you believe]
Credibility: [Who agrees, what guideline, what trial]
Action: [One clear thing to do next]
```

### Writing for WhatsApp Messages

WhatsApp is a personal channel. The copy must feel human, not robotic.

**Rules:**
- Max 3-4 sentences per "block"
- Use `*bold*` only for genuinely critical information
- Never use jargon without defining it
- End with a clear next step or question
- Never write a wall of text — break at logical units

**Good WhatsApp response opening:**
```
*CDI — First-line treatment*

IDSA/SHEA 2021 guidelines prefer fidaxomicin over vancomycin 
for initial non-fulminant episodes in adults [1].
```

**Bad WhatsApp response opening:**
```
According to the Infectious Diseases Society of America and the Society for 
Healthcare Epidemiology of America, in their 2021 focused update on the 
management of Clostridioides difficile infection, the preferred treatment...
```

### Writing for Investors

Investors are reading 50 decks a week. They skim. They are looking for: 
1. Is this a real problem?  
2. Is this a real market?  
3. Do these founders understand the problem better than anyone?

**Structure:**
```
1. The problem in one sentence (not two)
2. The market size — one credible number, sourced
3. Why now (what changed that makes this possible today?)
4. What you built (demo > description)
5. Traction (one real number beats 100 projections)
6. The ask (specific and simple)
```

### Writing Technical Documentation

Developers read docs to do a task, not to understand a system. Every doc page should be organized around what the user is trying to accomplish, not how the system works.

**Structure:**
```
# What This Does (1 sentence)
## When to Use It
## Quick Start (working code in 3 lines)
## Full API Reference
## Common Mistakes
## Examples
```

## Medical Evidence Summary Writing

When summarizing medical evidence, follow this structure:

### For Treatment Questions
```
[Drug/Intervention] is [recommended/supported/not recommended] 
for [condition] in [population] based on [evidence type].

Key evidence:
• [Specific finding 1] — [Source, Year]
• [Specific finding 2] — [Source, Year]

Clinical consideration: [One practical note relevant to Indian practice]

Confidence: [High/Moderate/Low] — [Brief reason]
```

### For Diagnostic Questions
```
[Test/Sign] has [sensitivity X%, specificity Y%] for [condition].

Key evidence:
• [Finding about the test] — [Source, Year]

Limitations: [One key limitation in Indian context]
```

## Common Failures to Avoid

### The Weasel Word Problem

These phrases mean nothing and destroy trust:
- "best-in-class", "cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art"
- "comprehensive", "robust", "scalable"
- "leverage", "utilize" (use "use")
- "going forward", "at the end of the day"
- "We are excited to announce..."

**Fix:** Replace every weasel word with a specific fact.

### The Corporate Voice Problem

Corporate voice is passive, hedged, and impersonal. It sounds like no one wrote it.

```
CORPORATE: "Our platform facilitates the retrieval of evidence-based clinical information."
HUMAN: "Ask a clinical question. Get a cited answer."
```

### The Assumption Problem

Never assume the reader knows:
- What your product does (explain it in the first sentence)
- Why they should care (connect it to their problem)
- What to do next (one clear call to action, not three)

### The Completeness Fallacy

More information ≠ better copy. A confused reader does nothing.

Write for the least-available moment of a doctor's day — running between patients, checking their phone for 30 seconds. If they can't understand your message in 30 seconds, rewrite it.

## Editing Checklist

Before finalizing any copy:

- [ ] Is the first sentence the most compelling sentence?
- [ ] Does every paragraph have one job?
- [ ] Are there any passive voice constructions? Rewrite active.
- [ ] Are there any weasel words? Replace with facts.
- [ ] Can you cut 20% more? Try.
- [ ] Read it aloud. Does it sound like a human wrote it?
- [ ] What's the one thing you want the reader to do after reading this? Is it obvious?
